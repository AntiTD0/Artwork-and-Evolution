Tabulating Machine

1890

The first data processing machine

A punch card is a piece of rigid paper containing digital information which is represented by the absence of holes predefined positions. These 17-centimetre (also called Hollerith cards) are used in semiautomatic data processing. The machine was completed in 1880 by Herman Hollerith, having 40 counters where each of them had a dial diveded into 100 divisions, and was processed by an electrical circuit. The population was growing rapidly, so the machine was needed for reading different kinds of data (like gender and age). The U.S.Constitution required a census every decade, so they counted the Americans population and got a number of 4 million people, but a century later the problem was huge. The population had grown rapidly and now the population was pretty much uncountable, and estimates warned that the 1890 census wouldn't be finished before 1900 census began, but then came Herman Hollerith who had the idea to record data on punched cards which would be read by a tabulating machine. Due to the machine essentials, it was a massive success and was used up to the 1950s.

Z1

1938

The first programmable computer

This motor-driven mechanical computer was designed by the computer scientist Kondrad Zuse in his parent's home from 1936 to 1938. The computer red instructions from pierced celluloid film, it was electrically driven mechanical calculator that had limited programmability. Z1 used Boolean logic and binary floating-point numbers where it was unreliable in action. Originally it was named "V1" for Versuchsmodell 1 which means Experimental Model 1, but after World War II it was renamed "Z1" to make a difference from the flying bombs designed by Robert Lusser. This machine overall consisted the control unit, micro sequences, memory, floating-point logic and input-output devices. The tape reader made this computer freely programmable, where it red perforations in 35-millimeter film. Kondrad have made models like Z2 and Z3 which were based on this model, they were also destroyed in WW2, but he still got his credits for creating the first programmable computer. For summary this 1 ton weighting, including 20,000 parts, remains as the first computer ever made.

ENIAC

1945

The first programmable digital computer

It was the first programmable, digital computer that was invented in 1943, by physicist John Mauchly and John Presper Eckert. This concept cost them $487,000 and the building took about 18 months where they decided to activate this heavy-weighted machine after the war was over, but no, the U.S military didn't care and put ENIAC to work. It showed weather predictions, commiting calculations for the hydrogen bomb design, random-number studies, cosmic-ray researches and wind-tunnel design. In 1946, Eckert and Mauchly developed the Electrical Numerical Integrator and Calculator which was called the "ENIAC". This was the machine that was essential to the American military, so they could use calculations for artillery-firing tables and other settings for target accuracy. These 270cm tall (9 foot) cabinet-like computers were fitted into a room that contained 40 of them. Inside the ENIAC were 17,468 vacuum tubes with 70,000 resistors, 1,500 relays, 6,000 manual switches, 10,000 capacitors and 5 million soldered joints. This comptuer's dimensions covering 167 square meters along with 30 tons of weight was literally a parked tank in a room. It took 160 kilowatts of electrical power, and to prevent overheating, was to develope two 20 horsepower blowers to deliver some cool air. At the time ENIAC was 1,000 times faster than any other calculators. This machine has got several improvements over the years, initially it could hold a 10-digit decimal number in memory, and later in 1956 they built 20 more of these calculators. Overall it was the first computer used, and was often called the "Giant Brain".

Point-contact transistor

1947

The first transistor

Transistor is a semiconductor device that contains at least 3 terminals to connect an electric circuit. Commonly the third terminal controls the flow of current which is between the other 2 terminals. Its used in radio recievers or for quick switching. Replacing the vacuum-tube, much larger and more power consuming thermionic valve, so this thing needed to reach it's goal. On December 1947 it was itroduced as the first transistor at Bell Laboratories which was in Murray Hill, New Jersey. The main idea of a field-effect transistor was designed by Julius Edgar Lilienfeld in 1925. After many attemtmps, failing with many different tools, the fully working one was invented by whole 3 research scientists, including John Bardeen, Willaim Shockley and Walter Brattain. They glued a small piece of gold foil to the edge of a triangular plastic wedge, a bit later one of them sliced the foil with a razor at the tip of the triangle, where the result was 2 very closely spaced contacts of gold. The plastic was put down to a crystal surface where the voltage said "yes" to the other side, ok I'm kidding but it applied which was now on the base of the crystal. As the base voltage pushed the electrons away from the base towards the other side located near the contacts when current started to flow from one contact to the other, and Immediately the point-contact transistor was invented.

JOHNNIAC

1954

The first engineering research computer

JOHNNIAC was one of the early computers built by the RAND Corporation. It was almost straightly operating for 13 years before finally shutting this machine down on February 11, 1966. Using 40-bit words and containing 1024 words of Selectron tube primary memory where each of them held 256 bits of data. Several improvements have been made to this computer over the years, where 4096 words of magnetic-core memory were added to the system which replaced the earlier Selectrons in March 1955. In 1955 this required 12 bits of addressing and had caused programs that stored data spare bits to fail, so a 12,000-word drum memory secondary storage system was put into this comptuer. In 1964 a real-time clock was added to support time-sharing. Now this machine is at the Computer History Museum that is located in the Mountain View city in California. For addition this whole machine weighted 2 tons with it's 5,000 vacuum tubes.

Intel 4004

1958

The first computer chip

Why it was needed? - Simple answer, building computers. Computer technology was already hard to understand since the 1940s, and now came the integrated circuit. Also known as a microship which is a set of electronic circuits on a small flat piece that is commonly made out of silicon, but sometimes semiconductor material. The chip includes mainly a large number of tiny transistors along with other small electronic parts. Chips are pretty much everywhere now, in memory, computer processors, 5G connectivity, artificial intelligence and virtual reality. Robert Noyce was the inventor of the monolithic integrated circuit which was finished in 1959. It was also made out of silicon. In the modern world IC chips are based on Noyce's monolithic IC, but the actual idea of a ingtegrated circle was made by Jack Kilby, so Robert Noyce doesn't remain as the first inventor, because he was the one who made it more buyable. Jack Kilby demonstrated the integrated circle on September 12, 1958, that had external gold-wire connections, so it made almost impossible to mass-produce, but he still remains the first creoter of a integrated circle and won the 2000 Nobel Prize in physics, invention of the integrated circuit. For summary Jack Kilby was the inventor, and Robert Noyce was who made it to mass-production.

COBOL

1959

One of the first popular programming languages

Its an English computer programming language that was built for business use, by CODASYL in 1959. FLOW-MATIC programming language designed by Grace Hopper - where the COBOL was based. Created as a piece of US Department of Defense effort, where it made a portable programming language for data processing. The statements have an English-like syntax where it was designed to be highly readable. COBOL overall had English syntax, for example "MOVE y to x", while the modern-day statements are easier "y = x;". The language wasn't also liked by many because of the verbosity, bad support for structed programming and design process, but its still being used for businesses actions in mainframes. The designers of the language were Jean E. Sammet, Norman Discount, Howard Bromberg, William Selden and Gertrude Tierney.

Sabre

1960

The first online reservation system

Often called Sabre Global Distribution System, is used by companies to price, book, search and ticket travel services which are provided by airlines, even car rentals and hotels, some times it was also used by travel agents. Developed by the CEO C.R. Smith who was from the American Airlines, in the game was the assistance of IBM in 1960. Later in 1976, the booking service became available for external travel agents. Originally its called "Semi-automated Business Research Environment" where it was spelled "SABRE". The American Airlines needed automatic booking reservations, so the magic needed to happen. Large growth in passenger volumes in the airline industry was a serious problem to American Airlines In the 1950s, because the booking system was entirely manual. About 8 operators needed to sort through a rotating file with cards per flight. When a seat was booked then the team would place a mark on the side of the card where they knew whether it was full or not. Usually writing up a ticket took 1.5 - 3 hours. In 1952 they introduced the Magnetronic Reservisor to replace the card files. This electromechanical computer consisting a single magnetic drum and every memory location was holding the number of seats left on specific flight, but at least 1 staff member was needed at each end of the line, where the ticket was difficult to handle. In 1953 Blair Smith was on an American Airlines flight where he found himself sitting next to American Lines president C.R. Smith. IBM along with the United States Air Force had been working on their Semi Automatic Ground Environment project (SAGE). SAGE originally used a large amount of computers to coordinate the message flow from radar sites to interceptors, so if an attack of a bomber was gonna come, they could fastly direct the bomber. The system what they used were the teleprinter machines that collect around the world information into the system. Also known as one of the first online systems, SABRE tought to use it for their booking system, and it was a success in 1960. Based on 2 IBM 7090 mainframes, the system was installed and cost $40 million at the time. Now their system could handle a huge number of transactions, like 83,000 phone calls per day.

This time a 3,000 punched cards were equal to 1 disk where it could easily store data on it. In 1973 the 8-inch IBM Diskette was another disk made by IBM, that could already hold 250 KB of data. There on, the disks were gaining a lot more data and yet more advanced because the compnay started to know the basics of a disk. In 1978, IBM wanted to reach $135 million for media and $875 million for drives, one disk cost $5 to 8.
